finding support

I thought I had breath support for a long time. I did not. I was singing every single day with tension I couldn't even feel ‚Äî tension in my throat, my chest, my jaw ‚Äî and I had no idea. No teacher to tell me. Just me, my voice, and YouTube videos that only go so far.

Bel canto is an old Italian style of singing. It means "beautiful singing." The whole idea is that your voice should sound effortless, free, like it costs you nothing. But getting there costs you everything.

The foundation of it is something called appoggio ‚Äî Italian for "support" or "leaning." It's not about pushing air out harder. It's actually the opposite. It's learning to hold back the air, to control the pressure from the bottom of your lungs outward so your voice has something stable to ride on. Like a balloon releasing air slowly and evenly instead of just popping.

I had no idea that was even a concept. I was just singing with chest tension and calling it effort. I plateaued for weeks and I didn't understand why my voice wasn't growing. That frustration was real. It started to feel personal, like maybe I just wasn't built for this.

So I started over from scratch. Farinelli breathing ‚Äî named after a famous castrato singer from the 1700s ‚Äî is a drill where you breathe in for a count, hold, breathe out for a count, hold, and repeat. The goal is building stamina and awareness of the breath cycle. I did it every morning. I also started semi-occluded vocal tract exercises, which basically means singing through a straw. It sounds ridiculous. It works because the resistance of the straw forces your vocal cords to find a more efficient way to vibrate without strain. It gives you feedback your ears alone can't give you.

I moved from chest breathing down to lower breathing, rib breathing. But I was still leaking air everywhere. Still not really supported.

Then one day something clicked. I felt my ribs expand outward and stay there while I was singing. Not just breathing in and collapsing ‚Äî actually holding the expansion. That was appoggio. That was what everyone was talking about. My whole body felt different. My notes felt like they had a floor under them for the first time.

I was sore after. Like muscle sore, in my sides and back. That scared me at first because you don't expect singing to feel like a workout. But it made sense once I understood I had basically never used those muscles before in my life.

That day I felt genuine anger and genuine happiness at the same time. Anger because it took this long, happiness because I finally found it. I remember just sitting there after practice not knowing what to do with that feeling.

breakdown to automation

Finding the technique wasn't the end. It was the beginning of the hardest part.

Because your body doesn't trust new muscle patterns. It doesn't matter that you figured it out once ‚Äî your nervous system doesn't make something automatic just because you did it correctly one time. You have to do it correctly hundreds of times. Then hundreds more. And in the beginning you can't even tell when you're doing it right versus when you're faking it and tensing up again.

So I stopped singing full songs. That decision hurt more than I expected. Singing had been my emotional regulation for years. Whenever something was hard or I was overwhelmed, I would sing. It was how I processed things. Taking it away, even temporarily, even for a good reason ‚Äî I didn't realize how much my body would react to that. It felt like losing a part of myself I didn't know I was depending on.

Early to late December was drills only. Straw exercises, Farinelli breathing, stability checks. I actually wrote some code to track whether my breath was staying steady during exercises. Just data. Just discipline. No songs.

January was the boring stage, and boring is actually the word I kept using. I knew what appoggio felt like. I knew the steps. My body just wasn't doing them automatically yet. Every session felt mechanical. I kept having to think through every breath consciously, which meant I couldn't focus on anything else ‚Äî not pitch, not tone, not expression. Just breathing correctly. Over and over.

A lot of people would have quit here. Honestly. Because there's no reward in this stage. You're not singing anything beautiful. You're not improving in any way that sounds good yet. You're just building a foundation in the dark and hoping it's the right one.

Mid to late January I cracked. One night after training I just fell apart emotionally. Not about singing specifically ‚Äî it was bigger than that. The discipline, the isolation of teaching yourself something this hard with no teacher to validate you, the months of uncertainty. It all came out at once. It felt like I was losing part of my identity. Like the version of me that just sang freely and emotionally was gone, and I didn't know yet what would replace it.

I went numb for about a week after that. Then something shifted. I stopped needing it to feel meaningful every session. I got neutral. That neutrality was actually the unlock ‚Äî because the emotional attachment to every practice session was part of what was keeping me tense. When I stopped needing each session to mean something, my body finally started to relax into the technique.

Late January into early February, support started showing up without me forcing it. Oo vowels became stable. Oh vowels became stable. Ahh is still inconsistent ‚Äî that one requires the jaw and the tongue to cooperate in a very specific way and it's still a work in progress.

I can lip trill through melodies now. A lip trill ‚Äî where you let your lips buzz together while you sing ‚Äî is basically impossible to do with tension. If you're gripping anywhere, the trill stops. The fact that I can sustain it through phrases means the support underneath is becoming real, not forced.

I started humming again just to stay musical. Little melodies throughout the day. Something I hadn't let myself do in months because I was afraid of reinforcing bad habits. Getting that back felt like getting something human back.

I'm not rushing back to full songs yet. I want to protect what I built. This whole journey taught me that consistency beats intensity every time ‚Äî less chasing the dopamine of a good session, more showing up every day even when it's quiet and boring and nothing feels special. That was the actual breakthrough. Not the technique. The mindset.
